When does YVR hit its limit?

Three lenses on capacity pressure at Canada's primary Pacific gateway: passenger volume versus infrastructure ceiling, runway utilisation against declared ATM capacity, and terminal load by segment.

Design capacity

YVR's current terminal complex (Main + South) was designed for ~25 million passengers/year. Since 2023 the airport routinely operates above this threshold.

Expansion plan

The DMUF (Domestic/International Main Upgrades & Future facilities) project targets a 30M passenger/year capacity. Completion expected ~2028–2030.

Runway constraint

YVR operates two parallel runways (08L/26R · 08R/26L) with a declared IFR capacity of ~70 ATM/hour. Peak-hour saturation is the binding operational constraint.

Already over capacity

YVR handled 27.9M passengers in 2024 — 112% of its 25M design capacity. The airport first exceeded 25M in 2018 and has operated in excess-capacity mode (except COVID years) ever since. Every gate, check-in desk, and baggage belt is running above its designed load.

30M ceiling: ~2027

At a conservative 3.5% CAGR — below the pre-COVID 4.3% and the post-COVID rebound rate — YVR reaches the planned expansion capacity of 30M passengers by 2027. The DMUF expansion targets completion in 2028–2030, meaning the gap will widen before infrastructure relief arrives.

COVID as false floor

The 2020–2021 collapse (−68% to 8.4M) masked underlying capacity pressure. The V-shaped recovery to 26.4M in 2023 happened in less than 2 years — faster than any infrastructure expansion cycle — confirming that the pre-COVID pressure was structural, not cyclical.

Noon peak: runway saturates

The 12:00 PDT slot carries scheduled arrivals, translating to an estimated total movements — of declared runway capacity. This is the operational chokepoint: a single ATC flow restriction, bad weather event, or cascading delay in this window propagates through the entire afternoon schedule.

Structural vs tactical mitigation

Peak-hour saturation cannot be solved tactically (schedule compression, gate management) beyond a certain point. A third runway — currently in early planning at YVR — would raise the declared capacity to ~95 ATM/hour, providing ~35% headroom at current peak volume. Without it, schedule growth is physically capped before passenger capacity is.

Analysis 3 · Terminal Pressure

International concourse under most structural pressure

YVR operates three distinct passenger zones: the Domestic concourse (D), the International/Transborder concourse (I), and the South Terminal. Flight frequency as a proxy for terminal load reveals asymmetric pressure — the International segment is growing faster while sharing the same terminal infrastructure.

Why International leads pressure

International and US Transborder passengers have longer processing times (customs, immigration, additional security) than domestic, multiplying the infrastructure load per movement. A 30% arrival share for International translates to disproportionately higher terminal dwell and processing burden — the primary driver behind the DMUF expansion scope.
